Plenty. The fun bit is usually **not** “train an LLM on images” in the abstract, but giving it a weird, useful job where image understanding and language actually work together.

Here are some strong creative directions.

**1. Visual taste mapper**
Train a system to look at rooms, outfits, posters, websites, cars, packaging, or landscapes and describe the *style DNA*:

* “minimal but warm”
* “2000s tech retail”
* “brutalist with luxury cues”
* “cheap-looking because of clutter and contrast”
  Then the LLM can explain *why* it thinks that, compare options, and suggest edits.

**7. Visual reverse-brief generator**
Upload an image and the model outputs the likely design brief behind it:

* target audience
* intended mood
* probable price point
* marketing strategy
* style influences
* functional priorities
  That’s genuinely useful for design study, moodboards, and competitor analysis.

**8. Environment personality classifier**
Classify spaces by behavioural traits:

* focus-friendly
* overstimulating
* cosy but impractical
* high-maintenance
* kid-safe
* accessibility-poor
* builder-grade
* premium but fragile
  Then the LLM explains what visual cues led to that conclusion.

**9. Prop and asset intelligence for games / 3D**
This one feels very you-adjacent.
Train on assets, scenes, or props to classify:

* realistic vs stylised
* hero asset vs background filler
* game-ready vs render-only
* texture issues
* topology risk indicators
* visual era / genre fit
  Then the LLM can act like an art lead giving review notes.

**10. Visual continuity checker**
For filmmaking, comics, ecommerce, or training content:

* shirt changed between frames
* object moved
* branding inconsistent
* lighting mismatch
* prop missing
* model pose continuity broken
  Great for production pipelines.

**13. Emotion of objects / spaces**
A poetic but surprisingly useful one.
Train on labels like:

* comforting
* tense
* lonely
* playful
* corporate
* handmade
* nostalgic
* sterile
  This could be used for branding, art critique, writing prompts, or recommendation engines.

**14. Learning companion for physical skills**
User uploads progress photos over time:

* drawing practice
* calligraphy
* cake decorating
* home gym form
* model painting
* guitar hand position
* sewing seams
  The system tracks progress and the LLM gives targeted coaching.

**15. “Explain what experts notice” model**
This is a killer concept.
Pick a field where experts see patterns others miss:

* architecture
* photography
* 3D modelling
* fashion
* typography
* landscaping
* automotive detailing
  Train the vision part to detect relevant cues, then the LLM translates expert perception into plain English.

**16. Trash-to-treasure detector**
Train on objects/spaces and classify:

* junk
* repairable
* collectible
* upcyclable
* salvage for parts
* worth cleaning
* probably not worth it
  This could be oddly addictive and very practical.

**17. Personal style memory system**
Instead of training on the whole internet, train on *one person’s preferences*:

* saved rooms
* liked product images
* favourite posters
* preferred outfits
* screenshots
* materials and colours
  Then the LLM becomes a creative assistant that actually understands that person’s taste.

**18. Mixed-media inspiration engine**
Input an image, output cross-domain inspiration:

* room image becomes a colour palette, outfit direction, poster design, and UI theme
* old machinery image becomes a game environment concept
* a plant photo becomes a packaging design system
  The LLM is great at that translation layer.

The strongest ideas usually have this shape:

**visual model** detects patterns
→ **structured features or labels**
→ **LLM** explains, critiques, recommends, or generates useful language

That tends to work better than trying to make the LLM itself do everything.

If you want something especially portfolio-worthy, I’d narrow it to one of these:

* **expert visual critic** for a niche hobby or profession
* **before/after improvement coach**
* **personal taste mapper**
* **repair triage assistant**
* **3D/game asset review assistant**

My hot take: the best creative projects are the ones where the model is not trying to be magical. It’s acting like a sharp observer with taste, memory, and language. That’s where it starts feeling genuinely useful instead of gimmicky.
